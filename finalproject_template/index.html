On June 24, 2022, the US Supreme Court overturned abortion rights with the reversal of Roe v. Wade. This decision then placed abortion policies and reproductive rights in the hands of each state. Roe v. Wade, the 1973 case that established a constitutional right to an abortion up until the fetus can survive outside of the womb, was the basis of ruling for nearly 50 years. But, the almost half-century old precedent has now been changed for good and has caused quite the commotion throughout the entire country.

Activists protest after Roe v. Wade is overturned Source: PBS

Following the Supreme Court's decision to reverse Roe v. Wade, regulations regulating the operation have taken effect in at least 13 states, where the majority of abortions are now illegal. Many of these laws forbid abortions before a woman is aware she is pregnant, at roughly six weeks into the pregnancy. However, in many places the legal battle over abortion access is still going on, with proponents filing lawsuits to stop the implementation of legislation that limit the practice. It is anticipated that almost half of the states will try to pass abortion restrictions or other gestational thresholds. While judges decide whether current or new restrictions can go into force, abortion is still permitted in some of these states for the time being. In the rest of the states, abortion is legal but may still be restricted, or access may otherwise be limited.

The number of legal abortions worldwide fell by more than 10,000 in the first two months following the Supreme Court's decision to overturn Roe v. Wade. This represents a fall of nearly 6%. Legal abortions in the thirteen states that were outlawed—mostly in the south—were severely restricted during those months and decreased to almost nothing. Major abortion restrictions were implemented in nine more states, and there was a 30% decrease in legal abortions in those nine states.

When compared to the baseline month of April, prior to the judgment, there were approximately 22,000 fewer abortions in the states with bans and limits in July and August. The number of abortions climbed by about 12,000, or 11%, in states where it remained allowed. That implies that almost 50% of women who were unable to obtain abortions in states with laws went to another state to do so. However, despite those increases, it appears that the new state legislation has stopped thousands of abortions.

Dr. Meg A. Frizzola of Nemours Hospital in Wilmington Delaware says that these findings “are not surprising.” States that have enacted bans have caused their citizens to travel to surrounding areas to seek the care they need and Frizzola notes that related hospitals have seen this change. According to Frizzola this issue has been one of the longest debates in medical history and it “seems like there will never be a solution that is going to make everyone happy.” She claims that as long as women are being taken care of properly and are receiving the medical attention that they deserve, that should be the only concern for doctors all over the country. “As someone who works in a legalized state, its feels good to be able to help women in need, because everyone deserves access to medical care.”

It's too early to analyze the long-term effects of the overturning of Roe v. Wade but changes are definitely being made in the way that women receive medical care surrounding pregnancy. However, with majority of the United States still allowing abortion to be legal, there is hope and assurance that the individuals who choose to have an abortion will have access to a safe and legal procedure.
